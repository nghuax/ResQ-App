ResQ Design Review

Recommendations to make the rescue journey faster, calmer, and easier to trust.

The current prototype already has strong foundations: a clear emergency trigger, compact mobile framing, and a usable end-to-end flow. The next design pass should focus on reducing decision time, increasing trust signals, and separating urgent user actions from operational fixer tasks more clearly.

Immediate
Clarify urgency hierarchy

Put emergency intent, ETA, and trust cues above browsing so stressed users can decide in seconds.

Core Flow
Make status more human

Track the person helping, what happens next, and when the user should act instead of showing raw steps alone.

Fixer Ops
Separate dispatch from history

Queue, SLA, and acceptance actions should dominate the fixer surface before secondary account functions.

Priority Improvements

P1 High impact on speed

Reframe the first screen around urgency

The current Services view feels like a catalogue first and an emergency tool second. In a breakdown scenario, users need instant reassurance, not a list they must interpret.

Recommended change Add a sticky emergency banner above services with current location, estimated nearest help, and two dominant choices: “Need urgent rescue” and “Book a service”. This reduces cognitive load and makes the product intent obvious.
P1 High impact on trust

Upgrade service and garage cards with proof

Cards currently show name, price, and distance, but they do not help users quickly judge reliability while under stress.

Recommended change Add verified badges, live availability, response confidence, and the strongest deciding metric in bold. Example: ETA first for emergency, price first for planned service, rating secondary.
P1 High impact on comprehension

Make order tracking feel more guided

The order screen has good detail, but the hierarchy is still information-heavy. Users should always know what is happening, who is responsible, and whether they need to do anything.

Recommended change Turn the top of the tracking screen into a live mission card with fixer identity, arrival window, and one explicit next step. Keep the timeline below as supporting detail rather than the primary element.
P2 Medium impact on navigation

Simplify the map and list choreography

The garage screen is visually interesting, but the user must mentally balance map markers, sheet expansion, vehicle filters, and phone actions all at once.

Recommended change Use a stronger selected state, persist the chosen garage in a compact sticky strip, and move comparison details into expandable rows so scanning and commitment happen in one focused area.
P2 Medium impact on operations

Redesign the fixer home around action density

The fixer flow is functional, but it behaves more like a generic app page than a live dispatch console.

Recommended change Elevate waiting jobs, response deadline, distance, and accept or decline actions into the first viewport. Keep account and historical content secondary so the screen works under operational pressure.
P2 Medium impact on accessibility

Increase legibility and one-hand usability

The visual language is clean, but some chips, meta text, and floating actions are still small for a real roadside context, especially outdoors and on moving devices.

Recommended change Increase type contrast, raise minimum tap targets, keep critical actions in the thumb zone, and make status colors more differentiated for active, pending, delayed, and completed states.
